— 01 / What Makes Seoul

Three things you'll never forget.

The qualities that make Seoul unlike any other capital — even compared to Tokyo or Bangkok.
01

A 24-hour city, in the kindest way

Subways run until midnight. Cafés stay open past 11pm. Convenience stores never close. The streets stay busy and well-lit, making late-night exploration feel safe rather than risky — even for solo travelers.

02

Every neighborhood has its own world

Hongdae is alive with student energy. Bukchon is hushed and historic. Gangnam glitters with luxury. Seongsu feels like Brooklyn's cooler cousin. Cross a single subway line and you've entered a different city.

03

Old and new, never in conflict

A Joseon-era palace next to an LG flagship store. Buddhist temples beside K-pop dance studios. Seoul doesn't choose between past and future — it lets them sit on the same street, somehow making both more vivid.

Jongno · 종로 · Heritage District
★ Heritage District

Where Seoul began.

종로 · The historic heart of the capital

For 600 years, this was the center of the Joseon Dynasty's universe. Five royal palaces stand within walking distance. Bukchon Hanok Village preserves traditional houses where people still live today. Insadong's narrow alleys overflow with tea houses, calligraphy shops, and traditional crafts. The pace here is intentionally slower — Jongno rewards the walker over the rusher.

Gyeongbokgung Palace
경복궁 · Grandest of the five royal palaces

Built in 1395, this is the largest and most iconic Joseon palace. The Geunjeongjeon throne hall and Gyeonghoeru pavilion (set on a lotus pond) are unmissable. Wear hanbok for free entry — rental shops cluster outside.

Bukchon Hanok Village
북촌한옥마을 · Living traditional village

A neighborhood of 900+ traditional Korean houses (hanok) on a hill between two palaces. Real residents live here — keep voices low and avoid flash photos near windows. The "8 Best Views" path is signposted; follow numbered markers for the iconic photo spots.

⏰ 24/7 outdoorFree📍 Quiet hours after 5pm

Insadong Street
인사동 · Traditional crafts & tea

A 700m pedestrian street lined with traditional craft shops, tea houses, and antique galleries. Even McDonald's and Starbucks here have signs in hangul only. Try a tea ceremony at any "전통찻집" sign — most include English explanation and traditional snacks.

Changdeokgung Palace & Secret Garden
창덕궁 · UNESCO World Heritage

Smaller and more intimate than Gyeongbokgung, but UNESCO-listed for its perfect harmony with nature. The Secret Garden (Huwon) tour is guided-only and limited to 100 people per slot — book online at least a few days ahead, especially on weekends.

⏰ 9am–6pm₩ 3,000 + ₩5k garden📅 Closed Mondays

Gwangjang Market
광장시장 · Korea's oldest market (1905)

Featured in Netflix's "Street Food" series. The famous food alley serves bindaetteok (mung bean pancake), mayak gimbap (mini seaweed rolls), and yukhoe (beef tartare) at communal tables. Bring cash — most stalls are cash-only. Best between 11am and 7pm.

Jogyesa Temple
조계사 · Heart of Korean Buddhism

A working Buddhist temple in the middle of downtown Seoul — visitors are welcome to enter the main hall (remove shoes). Free tea offered to all guests. Most beautiful in May during the Lotus Lantern Festival when thousands of paper lanterns hang overhead.

⏰ 4am–9pmFree🪷 Best in early May

Cheonggyecheon Stream
청계천 · Restored urban stream

An 11km restored stream running through downtown, once a paved highway. Stairs descend from street level into a different world — calmer, cooler, and lined with public art. Lit beautifully at night with seasonal installations. Walk a section, no need to do all 11km.

⏰ 24/7Free🌙 Best after sunset

Tongin Market
통인시장 · Brass coin lunchbox

A small traditional market with a unique system: buy ₩5k worth of brass coins (yeopjeon) at the center, then use them to "buy" small portions of food from various vendors to build your own dosirak (lunchbox). Locals love it; tourists rarely find it.

National Folk Museum
국립민속박물관 · Inside Gyeongbokgung

Often skipped because it's inside the palace grounds, but worth an hour. Walk through reconstructed traditional Korean houses, see how Joseon-era farmers and aristocrats lived. Free with palace ticket. English audio guide available.

⏰ 9am–6pmFree📅 Closed Tuesdays

Ikseondong Hanok Alley

Bukchon's cooler younger sibling. Traditional Korean houses converted into trendy cafés, cocktail bars, and design boutiques. Far less crowded than Bukchon, with the same hanok aesthetic but a more current energy. Best on a weekday afternoon for slow exploration.

Myeongdong · 명동 · Shopping District
★ Shopping & Beauty

The shopping capital of Asia.

명동 · Cosmetic and fashion district

Every major K-beauty brand has a flagship store within four blocks. Department stores tower over street vendors selling tornado potatoes and cheese-pulls. The streets pulse with shoppers from 11am until late evening. Tax-free counters are everywhere, and most clerks speak basic English, Mandarin, and Japanese. The street food scene is the cheapest gourmet experience in Seoul.

Hongdae · 홍대 · Youth & Live Music
★ Youth & Live Music

Where Seoul plays.

홍대 · Hongik University area

Named after Hongik University's prestigious art program, this neighborhood has been Seoul's youth culture epicenter for 30 years. Buskers play original songs on every corner from 7pm onwards. Photo booths line every street. Indie boutiques mix with chain cafés, club entrances with vintage shops. The energy itself is the attraction — there's no single landmark, just thousands of small experiences.

Gangnam · 강남 · Modern Luxury
★ Modern & Luxury

Wealthy, glittering, cinematic.

강남 · South of the river

Yes, the "Gangnam Style" Gangnam. Seoul's financial hub by day and a sleek nightlife district by night. Apartment buildings tower over wide boulevards. K-pop entertainment headquarters cluster here. Seongsu · 성수동 · Trendy Industrial
★ Trendy Industrial

The Brooklyn of Seoul.

성수동 · Once factories, now cafés

A decade ago, Seongsu was where shoes and leather goods were manufactured. Then artists moved into the cheap warehouses. Now those same brick buildings house Seoul's most photographed cafés, indie fashion brands, and pop-up stores that change weekly. Korean creators flock here to film. The aesthetic is industrial-meets-elegant — exposed brick, polished concrete, expensive minimalism.

Itaewon · 이태원 · International Quarter
★ International

Where the world meets Seoul.

이태원 · The international quarter

Originally home to expat military families during the Korean War era, Itaewon evolved into Seoul's most international neighborhood. Halal restaurants sit next to Mexican taquerias next to traditional Korean BBQ. Most signs are in English. It's the easiest neighborhood for non-Korean speakers, and the city's most diverse food scene. Hannam-dong (next door) has become Seoul's most luxurious quiet neighborhood.

— 04 / Suggested Itinerary

3 perfect days in Seoul.

A balanced route for first-time visitors. Mixes culture, food, and modern Seoul. Tap each day to expand.

Day One — Palaces & Tradition

하루 일정 · A walking-friendly day in Jongno
9:00 AM
Rent a hanbok near Gyeongbokgung
한복 대여 · ₩15–30k for 4 hours

Several rental shops sit just outside the palace. Wearing hanbok = free palace entry. Pose for photos in the courtyard before crowds arrive.

10:00 AM
Tour Gyeongbokgung Palace
경복궁 · Allow 1.5–2 hours

Catch the changing of the guard at 10am sharp. Walk slowly through the throne hall and royal residence. Free English audio guide at entrance.

12:30 PM
Lunch at Tosokchon
토속촌 · Famous samgyetang spot

Korean ginseng chicken soup, perfect after morning walking. Expect 20-min wait but worth it. ₩18k per person.

2:00 PM
Walk through Bukchon Hanok Village
북촌한옥마을 · Quiet residential area

Real people live here, so keep voices low. The "8 Best Views" path is signposted. Stop at any traditional café for tea.

4:30 PM
Insadong tea ceremony
인사동 전통찻집 · Cultural experience

Many tea houses offer English-explained ceremonies. Try Tea Story or Insadong Suyeon Sanbang. Expect ₩10–15k.

7:00 PM
Dinner at Gwangjang Market
광장시장 · Korea's oldest food market

Try bindaetteok (mung bean pancake), mayak gimbap, and yukhoe (beef tartare) if adventurous. Bring cash. ₩20–30k feeds two.

Day Two — Modern Seoul

현대적인 서울 · Contemporary culture and shopping
10:00 AM
Coffee in Seongsu
성수동 카페 · Onion or Daelim

Start at a former factory turned café. Order pandoro at Onion or daelim coffee at Daelim Changgo. Perfect Instagram fuel.

11:30 AM
Walk Seoul Forest
서울숲 · Urban green space

Adjacent to Seongsu. Has deer feeding area, bike rentals, and a small pavilion. 30 minutes is enough.

1:30 PM
Lunch & shopping in Garosu-gil
가로수길 · Tree-lined fashion street

Take subway to Sinsa Station. Browse concept stores and grab lunch at any healthy café. Mid-range budget ₩20k per person.

3:30 PM
SM Town Coex (or Lotte Tower)
코엑스 또는 잠실 · Pick one

K-pop fans go SM Town. Others can visit Starfield Library at Coex (free, photogenic) or Lotte Tower observation deck (₩30k).

7:00 PM
Korean BBQ in Gangnam
강남 고기집 · Try samgyeopsal

Pork belly grilled at your table. Most BBQ places have English menus and cook for you. Budget ₩30–40k per person with drinks.

9:30 PM
Drinks in Gangnam or Itaewon
강남 또는 이태원 · Nightlife

Gangnam for sleek lounges, Itaewon for international bar scene with English-speaking staff. Both vibrant until 2am.

Day Three — Local Seoul

현지인처럼 · Like a local, not a tourist
9:00 AM
Breakfast at Tongin Market
통인시장 · Lunchbox experience

Use brass coins (도시락) to buy small portions from various vendors. Build your own platter. ₩10k buys plenty for breakfast.

11:00 AM
Hike a small mountain
남산 또는 인왕산 · Pick easy trail

Namsan from Myeongdong takes 40 min and ends at N Seoul Tower. Inwangsan is wilder, 1.5 hr round trip. Both have city views.

2:00 PM
Lunch in Hongdae or Yeonnam
홍대 또는 연남동 · Local favorites

Hongdae for noodles or fried chicken. Yeonnam for indie cafés and brunch spots. Look for places packed with Korean students.

4:00 PM
Han River bike ride
한강공원 · Rent at Yeouido

₩3k/hour bike rentals everywhere along the river. Ride for 1–2 hours. Stop for instant ramen at convenience stores — a Seoul tradition.

7:00 PM
Last dinner: pojangmacha
포장마차 · Korean street tent bars

Try one in Jongno or Euljiro. Order soju with Korean pancakes (jeon) or odeng (fish cake soup). Most authentic Seoul night experience.

10:00 PM
Final stroll at Cheonggyecheon
청계천 · Stream through downtown

An 11km restored urban stream lit up at night. Walk a section, watch fountains. Perfect quiet end to a busy day.

— 05 / Seoul Food

Six dishes you must try.

Beyond the famous BBQ, these are dishes Seoul does better than anywhere else.
◉ Soup
Samgyetang
삼계탕 · Ginseng chicken soup

A whole young chicken stuffed with sweet glutinous rice, ginseng root, jujubes, and garlic, then slow-simmered for hours. Eaten traditionally in summer (the "fight heat with heat" philosophy), but its mild, restorative warmth works year-round. Comes with salt and pepper for seasoning, and a small cup of ginseng wine.

◉ Stew
Budae Jjigae
부대찌개 · Army base stew

A spicy stew born from the aftermath of the Korean War — Korean families used surplus American canned goods (Spam, sausage, baked beans) and combined them with kimchi, tofu, ramen noodles, and a fiery red broth. The result is comforting, nostalgic, and uniquely Korean. Cheese gets added at the end for richness.

◉ Street Food
Bindaetteok
빈대떡 · Mung bean pancake

Crispy outside, soft inside. Made from ground mung beans mixed with kimchi, mung bean sprouts, and pork, then pan-fried in generous oil. Best paired with Korean rice wine (makgeolli) — the slight bitterness of the wine cuts through the richness perfectly. A traditional Korean drinking food.

◉ Noodles

냉면 · Cold buckwheat noodles

Thin, chewy buckwheat noodles served in icy beef broth (mul-naengmyeon) or with spicy sauce (bibim-naengmyeon). Comes with mustard and vinegar — pour both in. A Pyongyang specialty that defected south during the war. Surprisingly refreshing year-round, especially after spicy food. Use scissors to cut noodles.

◉ BBQ
Mapo Galmaegi-sal
마포 갈매기살 · Pork skirt meat BBQ

A specific cut of pork (the diaphragm) found mostly in the Mapo neighborhood. More flavorful than samgyeopsal, less fatty, with a distinct chewy texture. Grill at your table with garlic, mushrooms, and onions. Wrap in lettuce and perilla leaves with ssamjang and rice. The Mapo specialty since the 1960s.

칼국수 · Hand-cut knife noodles

Hand-cut wheat noodles in clam, anchovy, or chicken broth. Served with kimchi, pickled radish, and a small bowl of warm rice on the side. — 06 / Seoul-Specific Tips

What guidebooks don't tell you.

Practical advice from locals and experienced travelers — the things you'll wish you knew on day one.
!
The subway is your best friend

9 lines, all color-coded, all in English. Buy a T-Money card at any station (₩4k). Last trains around midnight.

★
Free Wi-Fi everywhere

Seoul has free public Wi-Fi in subway stations, buses, and parks. Download "Seoul Wi-Fi" app for automatic connection.

✓
Use Naver Maps, not Google

Google Maps doesn't work well in Korea. Naver Map (English version available) gives accurate transit and walking routes.

◉
Restaurants close earlier than you think

Many restaurants stop seating at 9pm and close by 10pm. Plan dinner before 8pm or aim for late-night specific spots.

!
Avoid taxis in tourist areas

Use Kakao T app for taxis (link your card). Hailing in Myeongdong/Hongdae often results in inflated fares for tourists.

★
Last Wednesday is free at palaces

Culture Day — last Wednesday of every month. Free entry to all royal palaces and many museums. Crowds are bigger though.

✓
Tipping is not customary

Don't tip at restaurants, taxis, or hotels. Service charges are included. Leaving extra money can confuse staff.

◉
Avoid Friday/Saturday afternoon crowds

Myeongdong, Hongdae, and Insadong get extremely crowded. Visit Tuesday–Thursday for breathing room and shorter waits.
