Jinhae Cherry Blossom Festival

진해 군항제 · The largest cherry blossom festival in Korea
Over 340,000 cherry trees bloom across this naval port city, lining streets, train tracks, and the famous Yeojwacheon stream. The 10-day festival features parades, military band performances, and night illuminations. Arrive by KTX to Jinyeong Station, then transfer by bus.

Andong Mask Dance Festival

Hwacheon Sancheoneo Ice Festival

— 05 / Getting Around

Transport, simplified.

Korea has one of the world's most efficient public transport networks. Here's what you actually need to know.
Subway
지하철 · The backbone of city travel

Color-coded lines, English signage, and announcements. The Seoul Metro app is essential — works offline.

Tip · Avoid 8–9am rush hour
City Bus
시내버스 · Reaches where subway doesn't

Tap T-Money card on entry and exit. Blue buses run main routes, green buses are local, red buses are express.

Tip · KakaoMap shows live arrivals
KTX Bullet Train
고속철도 · Cross-country in hours

Seoul to Busan in 2.5 hours. Book through Korail or LetsKorail.com. Discounts available with the Korea Rail Pass.

Tip · Reserve seats on weekends
Taxi
택시 · Use Kakao T to avoid scams

Always use the Kakao T or Uber app — never accept "free" rides from strangers near tourist areas. Orange/silver are regular, black are deluxe.

Tip · Show address in Korean

— 07 / Frequently Asked

Questions every traveler actually asks.

Honest answers to the questions that don't always make it into guidebooks. Compiled from real visitor feedback.
No, tipping is not customary in Korea. In fact, leaving extra money can confuse staff or even cause embarrassment. Service charges are already included in the bill at most restaurants. The same applies to taxis, hotels, and salons.
팁 문화 없음 — 서비스 요금은 이미 포함되어 있습니다.
Yes, tap water in Korea is safe. It meets WHO standards and is regularly tested. However, most Koreans prefer bottled or boiled water due to old plumbing in some buildings. Restaurants serve free filtered water, and bottled water is cheap (₩500–1,000) at any convenience store.
Korea is one of the most card-friendly countries in the world. Credit and debit cards work almost everywhere — even at street food stalls and traditional markets. Visa and Mastercard are widely accepted. Bring a small amount of cash (₩50,000–100,000) for tiny vendors or temple donations, but you'll rarely need it.
카드 결제 가능 — Most places accept international cards.
Three options, all reliable: Pocket Wi-Fi rental (~₩5,000/day, pickup at airport), prepaid SIM cards (~₩30,000/week from KT or SKT), or eSIM (best for newer phones — buy online before arrival from Airalo or similar). Free Wi-Fi is also widely available in cafés, subway stations, and city centers.
Korea is among the safest countries for solo travelers, including women. Cities are well-lit and many areas remain busy until 2–3 AM. CCTV is everywhere. Common sense still applies — avoid empty alleys after midnight, and be cautious near nightlife districts where drunk patrons can be loud. The Tourist Police hotline (1330) is available 24/7 in English.
A few key things: Remove shoes before entering homes and many traditional restaurants. Receive items from elders with both hands. Don't write someone's name in red ink (associated with death). Don't stick chopsticks upright in rice (resembles funeral offering). When pouring drinks, pour for others first — never for yourself. Subway priority seats are strictly for elderly, pregnant, and disabled.
Korean food has spicy options, but plenty of mild ones too. Bibimbap, bulgogi, galbi, japchae, samgyeopsal, kimbap, and most soups are not spicy. Avoid anything with "매운" (spicy) in the name — like 매운 닭갈비. Ask "맵지 않게 해주세요" (not spicy, please). Even kimchi varies — some types are quite mild. White rice and water always cool things down.
맵지 않게 해주세요 — Maep-ji an-ke hae-ju-se-yo (Not spicy, please)
Yes — keep your receipts and passport. Tourists can claim VAT refunds on purchases over ₩30,000 at participating stores (look for "Tax Free" signs). Some stores process refunds instantly. For others, get the refund slip, then claim at airport kiosks before departure. Allow extra time at the airport for this — about 30 minutes.
Most places accept walk-ins, but a few require reservations. Book ahead for: Hallasan summit trails (Jeju), Lotte World Tower observatory (Seoul), some popular hanbok rental shops near palaces during weekends, and N Seoul Tower cable car. KTX trains and intercity buses also fill up on Friday evenings and holidays — book 1–2 days in advance to be safe.
It varies a lot by location. In Seoul tourist areas (Myeongdong, Hongdae, Itaewon), most service workers speak basic English. Outside major cities and tourist zones, English is limited. Younger Koreans (under 30) are usually more comfortable. Papago app (by Naver) translates better than Google Translate for Korean. Most signs in subways, airports, and tourist sites have English translations.
Tip — Download the Papago app before your trip.
